Attention drift during inline learning is a multi-dimensional phenomenon. Unlike traditional classroom settings where physical presence creates a social contract of engagement, asynchronous digital learning environments remove these external accountability mechanisms entirely.

Behavioural signals observable through browser APIs provide a rich proxy for cognitive engagement. Tab-switching frequency correlates strongly with task-switching costs described in cognitive load theory. Each switch introduces a re-engagement penalty of 15–30 seconds before deep focus resumes.

Mouse movement patterns carry surprising signal quality. Genuine reading behaviour produces micro-saccade-like mouse movements — small, irregular corrections as the reader tracks text. Passive consumption, by contrast, produces stillness or slow, pendulum-like drift.

Scroll velocity is perhaps the most underutilised signal. A learner actively processing content scrolls slowly and unevenly, pausing to re-read. A disengaged learner skims — producing burst-scroll patterns with sharp velocity spikes followed by long pauses.

Copy-paste events, while simple, are strong indicators of intent shift: the learner has moved from consumption to extraction or communication — potentially opening a chat window or email to share or ask about content.

The synthesis of these signals into a drift score must account for baseline variation. A naturally fast reader will exhibit higher scroll speeds than a slow one without being any less engaged. Personalised baselines, built over several sessions, are essential for reducing false positives.

Early interventions — gentle nudges rather than disruptive alerts — have shown efficacy in pilot studies. A subtle colour change in the browser chrome, or a soft audio cue, can re-anchor attention without breaking flow entirely.

The ethical dimension of attention monitoring in learning contexts cannot be overstated. Transparency about what is tracked, learner agency over their own data, and clear benefit-sharing (the data serves the learner, not the institution) are prerequisites for adoption.

Future directions include multimodal fusion: combining browser behaviour with webcam gaze estimation and audio-based distraction detection to create a genuinely holistic picture of learner presence.
